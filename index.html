Assignment 4 Writeup

Data: Abortion Data by U.S. State from the Guttmacher Institute

Data Proposition

States with the most significant reductions in available abortion clinics and resources experienced the most dramatic decreases in their residents' abortion rates between 2017 and 2020, and states with most dramatic abortion rate decreases were the ones with the most significant reductions in available abortion clinics.

Proof that Proposition is True

Design Rationale

  • Decision 1: I excluded a couple of outlier states--specifically, Kentucky and Oregon--by removing the specific data points from my plot. Deceptiveness: -0.5. I only excluded 2 outliers out of 51 states/territories, which is not a significant proportion. Also, excluding outliers is not very deceptive unless it severely skews the data, which it doesn't, since the trend line already had positive slope before I removed the outliers. However, this is still slight deception, because I chose to exclude the outliers not because they obscured the main message of the data but because they had a decrease in abortion rate despite a high increase in abortion clinics, meaning that they would disprove my claim--this means that I purposely removed datapoints that disprove my claim. This furthered the persuasiveness of my data while not being too obvious, since it's hard to notice 2 out of 51 datapoints missing unless one is directly counting--I hid the legend for states and didn't label all of the individual states to further conceal this choice. I could have not done this and my visualization would still have been quite persuasive, but I still chose to because it definitely improved the persuasiveness.
  • Decision 2: I labeled some points with especially large decreases in both abortion clinic number and abortion rates. Deceptiveness: 1.5. This decision didn't involve hiding, removing, or changing any data points--instead, all I did was label a few points that would further illustrate my proposition (since they have especially strong direct correlation). This choice helped illustrate my proposition well, since it shows that the states that have a large decrease in abortion clinic numbers also experience a decrease in abortion rates. However, this isn't fully earnest, since I only labeled these points and not the others. For example, I didn't label Arkansas even though the decrease in abortion clinic numbers is larger than that of Delaware and Iowa, which I included, because Arkansas saw an increase in abortion rates. Generally, I considered labeling all points with their values, but I decided not to because some other points don't prove my proposition as effectively (for example, one with a large decrease in one rate and a slight increase in another rate). (Also, doing so would clutter up the graph too much).
  • Decision 3: I highlighted the axes for both 0 abortion rate change and 0 abortion clinic number change. Deceptiveness: 2. This decision was completely not deceptive, since it highlighted axes without changing, hiding, or removing any data points or values. Also, even though it mainly furthers my proposition, it inadvertently highlights the fact that there are some points that show a negative correlation (increase in one rate, decrease in another), and I did not do anything to hide this. I chose to do this to give a better visual understanding of the four data quadrants--for example, the upper right and lower left (traditionally 1st and 3rd) quadrants are positive correlation between the rates/numbers, while the other two are negative correlation. It's clear that most points are in the 1st and 3rd quadrants, but unfortunately this visualization shows that there are some in the other 2 quadrants. This isn't theoretically a disproof of my proposition, since I did not say that the changes were fully directly correlated, and more that large decreases in one are associated with large decreases in another. Highlighting one axis but not the other could maybe hide this fact better, but I chose to accentuate all quadrants (instead of half-planes) because the benefits of proving my proposition outweigh the minor drawbacks of not having a strong direct correlation.

Proof that Proposition is False

Design Rationale

  • Decision 1: I plotted the decreases and increases as if they were along the same axis, even though the positive and negative parts of the horizontal axis represent different values. Deceptiveness: -1. I did not fake, hide, or remove any data by doing this. However, I put two otherwise non-equivalent axes together to visually further the idea that the changes are always in opposite directions (and therefore it's not true that overall abortion-seeking rates of residents decrease as well), which isn't technically valid since two different axis shouldn't be conjoined. One could put the two bars side-by-side in a way that uses different axes, or negate one of the rates so that both of them are positive. Despite that, I still stand by this choice, since it wouldn't be questioned by the typical audience--it makes sense at first glance since both are percentage changes. It also provides the most intuitive visualization of the inverse relation.
  • Decision 2: I highlighted the middle line (which happens to be both the 0 value and the dividing line between the two horizontal axes). Deceptiveness: 2. First of all, I did not change or remove data by highlighting this middle line. I simply indicated the point that simultaneously represents 0 change and also the point that divides the % change in abortion clinics axis from the change in out-of-state abortions axis. This is not a deceptive action at all--in fact, one could argue that this actually may point the audience towards noticing that the two axes are different (and possibly should not be connected), but I chose to highlight it regardless because this choice helps differentiate between the two bars for each state from each other (along with the fact that the two bars have different colors), which in turn demonstrates how one rate's decrease is directly accompanied by another's increase.
  • Decision 3: I used the same scale for the two % changes (opposite sides of the horizontal axis) even though they represent different things. Deceptiveness: -0.5. This choice did not change the data in any way. However, the use of identical axis ranges (both of size 100, starting at 0 and going to + or - 100) for both the abortion clinic number changes and out-of-state abortion changes, which improves balance and facilitates better comparison of values, is slightly questionable since the two metrics measure different types of things and have different ranges of values. The lack of individual optimization may allow the audience to mentally conflate the two values as being more similar (for example, both measuring changes in population counts for certain things) than they actually are. However, the use of a common scale minimizes visual distortion and allows for better magnitude comparison, and the values aren't being modified, so the deception is very minor. Using scales more optimized for each value (in this case, the range would be smaller for the left side than the right side) would provide a more accurate display of absolute magnitude, but sacrifices the important visual comparison that leads to the crucial conclusion: decrease in abortion clinics doesn't discourage residents from getting abortions, it drastically increases out-of-state abortion care seeking.

Final Thoughts

Most of the deceptive / data visualization process was not too difficult. Once I viewed the data and did explorary data analysis, I noticed patterns between changes in abortion clinic rates, in-state abortion rates, and out-of-state abortion rates. I quickly formulated my claim, then the way to prove and disprove it came to light quickly as well: to prove it, I would plot the direct correlation between abortion clinic rates and in-state abortion rates, while I would focus on the fact that out-of-state abortion rates increased to disprove the idea that less abortion clinics discourages residents from seeking abortion care altogether. It was challenging to grapple with outliers and other data properties/values that went against my desired main claims; it was also hard to determine which deceptive visualization techniques were acceptable. I chose to look at the data from a new perspective multiple times to determine if my deception was too "obvious", which was definitely beneficial to my decision making. In terms of ethics, my main belief was that I didn't change the data values or exclude too much data, so it was acceptable. The most surprising part was how one small act of deception can go a long way towards improving my visualization.

Overall, this process was intuitive, yet challenging. It was also very eye-opening, since it demonstrated how small acts of deception can go a long way towards improving visualization, by accentuating properties that support claims and filtering evidence against these claims. It raises the important question of whether deceptive visualization can ever be ethical. At first glance, deception is never ethical, but this isn't necessarily true--for example, if a visualization creator accentuates certain properties of data without changing or removing other values, and this draws attention to an important flaw that leads to actions being taken, the overall visualization is certainly viewed as ethical. A tentative criteria is: ethical analysis and visualization doesn't aim to hide, but if deceptive techniques are required to accentuate values, then they're acceptable if used in moderation. Here, moderation means there is not too much data modification (for example, removing 1 outlier could be useful if that outlier skews the data while not being semantically important, but removing a whole group of data points is not acceptable).
